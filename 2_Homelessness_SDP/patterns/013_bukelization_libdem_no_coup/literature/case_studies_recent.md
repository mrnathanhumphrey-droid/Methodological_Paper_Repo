# Case Studies: Civilian-Legal Authoritarian Consolidation (Bukelization)

Literature fetch covering 8 cases of libdem-index collapse without coup or interstate war: SLV, HUN, TUR, VEN, POL, TUN, BLR, IND. Focus: recent (2020-2026) peer-reviewed empirical work documenting institutional capture sequence, trigger events, key actors, resistance, and surviving opposition.

Compiled 2026-05-25. Citations are from real publications surfaced via Google Scholar / publisher pages; paywalled abstracts noted where applicable.

---

## SLV (El Salvador, Bukele 2019-)

### Trigger events
- **Feb 2020**: Bukele enters Legislative Assembly with armed soldiers to pressure security-package vote ("9-F").
- **Feb 2021**: Nuevas Ideas (NI) wins legislative supermajority (56/84 seats).
- **May 1, 2021**: New NI-controlled Assembly removes 5 Constitutional Chamber magistrates + Attorney General Raúl Melara on opening day, replaces with loyalists.
- **Sept 2021**: Reconfigured Constitutional Chamber rules immediate re-election permissible (overturns longstanding ban).
- **March 27, 2022**: State of exception declared after gang-killing spike; renewed monthly ever since. ~85,000 detentions, ~110,000 total prison population.
- **Feb 2024**: Bukele re-elected with ~85% in election the prior court would have blocked.

### Captures, in order
1. **2021 Legislature → Judiciary (Constitutional Chamber)**: Statutory dismissal of 5 magistrates with no due process, replaced same day. Mechanism: simple majority vote in Assembly, claiming "manifest causes."
2. **2021 Attorney General**: Same-day removal of Melara, replacement with Rodolfo Delgado.
3. **2021 Lower judiciary**: "Judicial career law" reforms forced retirement of ~one-third of judges over 60 / with 30+ years service — purged ~200 judges en masse.
4. **2022 Civil society / media**: Foreign Agents Law proposals + Pegasus surveillance of El Faro journalists (documented by Citizen Lab); state-of-exception suspends due process / association rights.
5. **2024 Electoral law**: Redistricting consolidates 84 → 60 seats; municipalities consolidated 262 → 44. NI wins 54/60.

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Meléndez-Sánchez, M. (2021). "Latin America Erupts: Millennial Authoritarianism in El Salvador."** *Journal of Democracy* 32(3): 19-32. Introduces "millennial authoritarianism" — populist appeals + classic autocratic moves + polished social-media brand. Frames Bukele as a templated case, not idiosyncratic.
- **Meléndez-Sánchez, M., & Vergara, A. (2024). "The Bukele Model: Will It Spread?"** *Journal of Democracy* 35(3). Argues the crackdown's success rests on (a) intensity and (b) an unintended consequence of a covert pact between gangs and the government. Key for understanding the "popularity-as-shield" mechanism.
- **Meléndez-Sánchez, M. (2024). "How the World's Most Popular Dictator Wins."** *Journal of Democracy* (online). Empirical decomposition of 2024 vote: high turnout + opposition fragmentation + redistricting + Constitutional-Chamber-blessed re-election.
- **Gellman, M. (2024).** Multiple analyses (incl. CU Boulder Political Science) arguing El Salvador no longer meets benchmarks for liberal democracy — press freedom, rule of law, basic rights.
- **Cambridge LARR (2025). "Hidden in Plain Sight: The In/Visibility of Human Rights in El Salvador's Prisons Under the State of Exception."** *Latin American Research Review.* Empirical: prison conditions, torture allegations, deaths in custody.

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **El Faro** (investigative outlet) — moved operational base to Costa Rica 2023 after surveillance + tax harassment. Continues publishing.
- **Cristosal** (human rights NGO) — documents state-of-exception abuses; relocated leadership to Guatemala 2025 after charges filed.
- **US response**: Treasury sanctioned Bukele insiders for gang-pact corruption (Marvin Reyes, Carlos Marroquín). Largely symbolic; no aid cutoff.
- **IACHR / UN OHCHR** statements; no effective regional bloc pressure (OAS captured by Mexico-Brazil left + Bukele's regional popularity).

### Open empirical questions
- Will Bukele attempt 2029 re-re-election (Constitutional Chamber already ruled once)?
- Does the gang-pact thesis survive scrutiny if NI documents are ever obtained?
- What is the actual incarceration-rate ceiling before economic productivity is impaired?

## HUN (Hungary, Orbán 2010-)

### Trigger events
- **April 2010**: Fidesz wins 52.7% → 2/3 supermajority via electoral-system mechanics.
- **April 2011**: New "Fundamental Law" enacted by simple supermajority (no referendum) — replaces 1989 constitution.
- **2013**: Fourth Amendment to Fundamental Law rolls back Constitutional Court decisions retroactively.
- **2014, 2018, 2022**: Three more 2/3 supermajorities under redistricted system + diaspora-vote rules.
- **2020**: COVID emergency law allows rule-by-decree (briefly).
- **2022**: European Parliament resolution declares Hungary no longer a "full democracy" — formal label "electoral autocracy."

### Captures, in order
1. **2010-2011 Constitutional Court**: Expanded from 11 to 15 justices, term lengthened to 12 years, all new appointments controlled by Fidesz supermajority. Fourth Amendment (2013) strips Court of jurisdiction over budget questions and pre-2012 jurisprudence.
2. **2010-2014 Electoral system**: Redistricted to favor Fidesz rural strongholds; mail ballots from ethnic-Hungarian diaspora (overwhelmingly Fidesz) added; opposition campaign finance constrained.
3. **2010-2015 Media**: Media Council (5 Fidesz appointees, 9-year terms) created 2010. State broadcaster (MTVA) made loyal. Klubrádió frequency revoked 2021. Private outlets (Origo, Index, Népszabadság) captured/closed by Fidesz-aligned oligarchs.
4. **2010-2018 Civil society**: "Foreign-funded NGO" law (2017); CEU forced to Vienna (2018); academic-research-network MTA stripped of institutes (2019).
5. **Ongoing judiciary**: National Judicial Office (OBH) created with extensive powers over case assignment / judge appointment, headed by Tünde Handó (Fidesz-affiliated).

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Bozóki, A., & Hegedüs, D. (2018). "An externally constrained hybrid regime: Hungary in the European Union."** *Democratization* 25(7): 1173-1189. Canonical: the EU constrains but does not reverse the Orbán regime. Still the most-cited frame as of 2024.
- **Enyedi, Z. (2024). "Concept-stretching and the case of Hungary's well-managed autocratization."** Term "well-managed autocratization" enters the literature. Argues Orbán's regime is distinctive in technocratic competence, not just illiberalism.
- **Krekó, P., & Enyedi, Z. (2018). "Explaining Eastern Europe: Orbán's Laboratory of Illiberalism."** *Journal of Democracy* 29(3). Early synthesis; still anchor citation.
- **Bogaards, M. (2018). "De-democratization in Hungary: diffusely defective democracy."** *Democratization* 25(8): 1481-1499.
- **V-Dem Institute (2023, 2024, 2025, 2026) Democracy Reports** — Hungary consistently flagged as top-10 autocratizer; only EU member ever classified "electoral autocracy."

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **Péter Magyar / TISZA party** (emerged 2024 from Fidesz internal split). EP elections 2024: TISZA 30% vs Fidesz 44.6%. First credible electoral challenger since 2010.
- **EU rule-of-law conditionality** (Article 7 since 2018; ~€22B frozen). Forced minor judicial concessions 2023-24 but no rollback.
- **Independent media (Telex, 444, HVG, Magyar Hang)** survive but circulation/reach constrained.
- **Budapest mayor Karácsony** + opposition-held large cities = enclave resistance.

### Open empirical questions
- Will TISZA's 2026 election challenge be neutralized by electoral system mechanics, or will it succeed?
- If Orbán loses, can institutional capture be unwound — or is Fundamental Law structurally entrenched?
- How much of Hungary's case is *causally* EU-constrained vs. would have looked the same outside EU?

## TUR (Turkey, Erdoğan 2003-)

### Trigger events
- **2002**: AKP wins single-party majority on 34% vote (10% threshold mechanics).
- **2007**: Constitutional referendum makes presidency directly elected.
- **2010**: Constitutional referendum (58% yes) expands HSYK (judicial council) — court-packing window opens. **Esen-Gümüşcü identify 2010 as the regime's pivot moment.**
- **2013 Gezi protests**: violent suppression begins media closure cycle.
- **2016 attempted coup**: triggers 2-year state of emergency, purge of ~150,000 from public service, ~3,000 judges/prosecutors dismissed, ~190 media outlets shuttered.
- **April 2017 constitutional referendum** (51.4% yes, contested): replaces parliamentary with presidential system. Takes effect 2018.
- **2023 elections**: Erdoğan wins second-round despite economic crisis + earthquake response failure.

### Captures, in order
1. **2007-2010 Judiciary**: HSYK reform via 2010 referendum expands council, alters selection. Court-packing of Constitutional Court (Anayasa Mahkemesi) follows.
2. **2010-2016 Police/military**: Ergenekon + Sledgehammer trials decimate Kemalist military officer corps; later reversed under Gülenist-conspiracy framing post-2016.
3. **2016-2018 Mass purge**: State-of-emergency decrees (KHK) dismiss tens of thousands; judiciary, academia, media, civil service rebuilt with loyalists.
4. **2017-2018 Constitution**: Presidential system removes prime minister, parliament weakened, president gains decree power and appointment authority over Constitutional Court (4 of 15 justices, plus indirect via expanded HSK).
5. **2019-2024 Local government**: Opposition mayors (İmamoğlu, Yavaş) elected 2019 + 2024; İmamoğlu jailed March 2025 (per *Jacobin* 2025) on graft charges widely seen as political.

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Esen, B., & Gümüşçü, Ş. (2016). "Rising competitive authoritarianism in Turkey."** *Third World Quarterly* 37(9): 1581-1606. The foundational classification.
- **Esen, B., & Gümüşçü, Ş. (2023). "How Erdoğan's populism won again."** *Journal of Democracy* 34(3). Post-2023-election analysis: elections competitive but unfair; playing field highly skewed; civil liberties violated systematically.
- **Esen, B., & Gümüşçü, Ş. (2024). "Competitive yet unfair: May 2023 elections and authoritarian resilience in Turkey."** Empirical post-mortem on 2023.
- **Ugur-Cinar, M. (2023). "Elections and Democracy in Turkey: Reconsidering Competitive Authoritarianism in the Age of Democratic Backsliding."** *The Political Quarterly* (online first).
- **Cebeci, A., & Üstündağ, N. (2024). "Judicial transformation in a competitive authoritarian regime: Evidence from the Turkish case."** *Law & Policy* (online). Detailed mechanism account of 2010-2016 court-packing.
- **V-Dem (2024) Democracy Report**: Turkey listed among top autocratizers; large declines on judicial autonomy and media freedom.

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **CHP + İYİ + HDP/DEM** opposition coalition (Millet İttifakı) — fragmented but persistent.
- **Istanbul / Ankara metropolitan governance** — opposition controls both since 2019.
- **Independent media (Cumhuriyet, BirGün, Medyascope, +24 small outlets)** — circulation/digital reach significant; physical newspapers heavily constrained.
- **Constitutional Court** — has issued surprise pro-rights rulings (Demirtaş, Kavala) but rulings ignored by lower courts in some cases.

### Open empirical questions
- Will the 2028 election (Erdoğan's term limit, contested) be held? Under what rules?
- Can the post-2016 KHK purges be unwound legally?
- What is the cumulative economic cost of institutional capture (lira crisis, FDI flight)?

## VEN (Venezuela, Chávez 1999 - Maduro 2013-)

### Trigger events
- **1999**: Chávez elected; Constituent Assembly drafts new constitution (Dec 1999 referendum).
- **2002 coup attempt** against Chávez (48 hours); aftermath consolidates loyalty in military.
- **2004**: Court-packing — Supreme Tribunal of Justice (TSJ) expanded from 20 to 32 justices via Organic Law of the TSJ; all new appointments Chavista.
- **2009 referendum**: removes presidential term limits.
- **2013 Maduro succession** post-Chávez death (Apr 2013, narrow win contested).
- **2015 December**: opposition wins legislative supermajority; outgoing Chavista National Assembly packs TSJ "express justices" (lame-duck packing).
- **2016-2017**: TSJ strips National Assembly of powers; 2017 Constituent Assembly elected without opposition.
- **2018, 2024**: presidential elections widely declared fraudulent; 2024 vote tallies suppressed.

### Captures, in order
1. **1999-2000 Constitution**: Bolivarian Constitution drafted by Chavista-dominated Constituent Assembly; ratified by referendum.
2. **2004 Judiciary**: Expansion of TSJ from 20 to 32 justices via simple-majority Organic Law; all new appointments Chavista. This is the foundational autocratic-legalism move.
3. **2004-2010 Media**: RCTV license non-renewal (2007). Broadcast law tightens.
4. **2009 Term limits**: Constitutional referendum removes all elective-office term limits.
5. **2015-2017 Legislature**: When opposition wins Assembly, TSJ rules its actions void; 2017 Constituent Assembly created in extra-constitutional vote, supersedes Assembly.
6. **2018-2024 Elections**: Opposition candidates banned (Capriles, Machado); CNE controlled by Chavismo; 2024 ballot tallies never released.

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Corrales, J. (2015). "Autocratic Legalism in Venezuela."** *Journal of Democracy* 26(2): 37-51. **Foundational term: "autocratic legalism."** Still the most-cited frame.
- **Corrales, J. (2022). *Autocracy Rising: How Venezuela Transitioned to Authoritarianism.*** Brookings Institution Press. The book-length synthesis. Explains Maduro's survival via (a) asymmetric party-system fragmentation, (b) institutional destruction + colonization ("autocratic legalism"), (c) institutional innovation ("functional fusion").
- **Corrales, J., & Penfold, M. (2011/2015). *Dragon in the Tropics: Hugo Chávez and the Political Economy of Revolution in Venezuela.*** Brookings.
- **Mainwaring, S., & Pérez-Liñán, A. (2023). "Venezuela's Autocratization, 1999-2021: Variations in Temporalities, Party Systems, and Institutional Controls."** In *When Democracy Breaks* (Oxford Academic). Periodizes the autocratization; party-system collapse is central causal mechanism.
- **Cambridge "Resisting Backsliding" volume (2022), Ch. 4: "Chávez Vete Ya: The Erosion of Democracy in Venezuela."**

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **María Corina Machado** (Vente Venezuela) — disqualified from 2024 ballot but mobilized opposition machinery; Edmundo González stand-in.
- **2024 election**: opposition collected actas from ~80% of mesas, published them; widely accepted as showing González won 67% / Maduro 30%.
- **External**: Lima Group, OAS, sanctions regime — none have produced regime change.
- **Civil society / Foro Penal**: documents political prisoners (~1,800+ as of 2025).

### Open empirical questions
- Does Maduro's 2024 stolen election mark a new equilibrium or trigger collapse?
- What is the role of Cuban / Russian / Iranian security advisers in regime persistence?
- How does autocratic legalism literature translate to oil-dependent vs non-rentier cases?

## POL (Poland, PiS 2015-2023; recovery 2023-)

### Trigger events
- **Oct 2015**: PiS wins legislative majority (37.6% vote → majority via Polish electoral mechanics).
- **Nov-Dec 2015**: Constitutional Tribunal crisis begins — PiS refuses to seat 3 judges named by outgoing Sejm, names own 3, packs Tribunal.
- **Dec 2015**: Tribunal-paralysis law (must hear cases in chronological order).
- **2017-2018**: Supreme Court reform; "muzzle law" on judges (2019).
- **Oct 2021**: Tribunal rules EU law contrary to Polish Constitution.
- **Oct 2023**: Opposition coalition (KO + Trzecia Droga + Lewica) wins majority; Tusk forms government Dec 2023.
- **2024-2026**: Recovery period; legal mechanism for de-capture still contested.

### Captures, in order
1. **2015-2016 Constitutional Tribunal**: Refusal to seat predecessor's judges + packing of own + chronological-order paralysis law. **First and decisive move.**
2. **2017-2018 Supreme Court**: New Disciplinary Chamber created (later ruled non-independent by CJEU); forced retirement of one-third of justices via lowered retirement age; reversed under EU pressure but selection still PiS-controlled.
3. **2017-2019 National Council of the Judiciary (KRS)**: politicized via parliamentary appointment of judicial members.
4. **2016- Public media (TVP, Polskie Radio)**: turned into PiS propaganda outlets via "small media law" (Dec 2015) giving Treasury Minister direct control.
5. **Prosecution service**: Minister of Justice (Ziobro) made Prosecutor General simultaneously — formal fusion of political and prosecutorial power.

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Sadurski, W. (2019). *Poland's Constitutional Breakdown.*** Oxford University Press. The book-length account. **Three-dimensional framework**: (a) concentration of political power in ruling-party leader, (b) violation of constitutional norms, (c) amendment of constitution via ordinary laws without constitutional majority.
- **Sadurski, W. (2020). "Polish Constitutional Tribunal Under PiS: From an Activist Court, to a Paralysed Tribunal, to a Governmental Enabler."** *Hague Journal on the Rule of Law*.
- **Castillo-Ortiz, P. (2023). "Judicial transformation: The case of the Polish Constitutional Tribunal."** *European Journal of Political Research* — Cambridge Core. Mechanism-focused empirical account.
- **Bill, S., & Stanley, B. (2020). "Whose Poland is it to be? PiS and the struggle between monism and pluralism."** *East European Politics* 36(3).
- **Pirro, A., & Stanley, B. (2022). "Forging, Bending, and Breaking: Enacting the 'Illiberal Playbook' in Hungary and Poland."** *Perspectives on Politics* 20(1).
- **Bernhard, M. (2024). "Democracy After Illiberalism: A Warning from Poland."** *Journal of Democracy* (online) — analysis of Tusk-era recovery challenges.

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **Komitet Obrony Demokracji (KOD)** + Black Protests + Strajk Kobiet (women's strike 2020 over abortion ruling).
- **Independent media (Gazeta Wyborcza, TVN, OKO.press)** survived as ownership stayed private (TVN under US-owned Warner Bros Discovery).
- **EU Article 7 + CJEU rulings + €36B RRF conditionality** — pressure tightened 2021-2023, contributed to 2023 turnover.
- **Local government** — large cities (Warsaw, Gdańsk, Poznań) opposition-held throughout PiS era.
- **2023 opposition coalition victory** is the empirical exemplar of *reversible* backsliding.

### Open empirical questions
- Can Tusk de-capture Constitutional Tribunal without violating same norms PiS violated? (Ongoing 2024-2026 constitutional dispute.)
- President Duda (PiS-aligned) blocks legislation via veto until 2025 election — does the recovery survive?
- How transferable is the Polish reversal model to other captured states?

## TUN (Tunisia, Saied 2021-)

### Trigger events
- **2019**: Saied elected with 72.7% in runoff (anti-establishment outsider, no party).
- **2020-21**: political deadlock between Saied, PM Mechichi, Speaker Ghannouchi (Ennahda).
- **July 25, 2021**: Saied invokes Article 80 (emergency powers), dismisses PM Mechichi, suspends Parliament, lifts MP immunity. Widely called a "self-coup."
- **Sept 22, 2021**: Decree 117 — Saied rules by decree, suspends most of 2014 constitution.
- **March 2022**: Dissolves Parliament formally.
- **July 25, 2022**: Constitutional referendum on new constitution (94.6% yes, ~30% turnout). New constitution: hyper-presidential, no parliamentary check, judicial independence stripped.
- **Sept 2022**: New electoral law (decree) replaces party-list with individual candidacies.
- **Dec 2022 / Jan 2023**: Parliamentary elections; ~11% turnout in first round, ~11.4% in second.
- **2023-2024**: Opposition leaders arrested (Ghannouchi, Chebbi, Hamadi); presidential election Oct 2024 — Saied 90.7%, two opposition candidates jailed or barred.

### Captures, in order
1. **July 25, 2021 Parliament + Government**: Article-80-pretextual self-coup. **No Constitutional Court existed to block** (parliament had failed to seat one since 2014 — empirical critical-juncture explanation).
2. **Sept 2021 Decree 117**: rule-by-decree; suspends 2014 constitution.
3. **Feb 2022 Supreme Judicial Council**: dissolved by Saied; 57 judges fired Jun 2022.
4. **July 2022 Constitution**: hyper-presidential, no judicial review of presidential acts, religious-state language inserted.
5. **2022-2023 Electoral system**: party-list → individual candidacies destroys Ennahda's mobilizational advantage.
6. **2023-2024 Opposition**: leaders jailed under broadly-defined plot/conspiracy charges; media restricted under Decree 54.

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Yerkes, S., & McCormack, S. (2021). "Coup in Tunisia: Is Democracy Lost?"** *Journal of Democracy* (online). The early authoritative classification.
- **Tamburini, F. (2023). "'How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love Autocracy': Kais Saied's 'Constitutional Self-Coup' in Tunisia."** *Journal of Asian and African Studies* 58(7): 1219-1234. **Empirical anchor on the legal mechanism.**
- **Cordall, S., et al. (2023). "Breakdown by disengagement: Tunisia's transition from representative democracy."** *Conflict, Security & Development* / *Global Discourse* (Taylor & Francis, doi 10.1080/2474736X.2023.2279778). **Argues Tunisia diverges from standard backsliding model: not executive aggrandizement against an organized opposition, but collapse-by-disengagement.** Mair-inspired framework.
- **Boubekeur, A. (2023). "The US and EU Response to Tunisia's Democratic Backsliding: Promoting Democracy or Protecting Interests?"** *The International Spectator* 59(3).
- **Arab Center DC, Doha Institute studies (2022-2024)** — descriptive but well-documented chronologies.

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **Ennahda** — leadership jailed (Ghannouchi sentenced repeatedly, 2024). Party structure largely dismantled.
- **Front of Salvation** — opposition coalition; key figures (Néjib Chebbi) arrested.
- **UGTT trade union** — largest civil-society actor; ambivalent (criticized Saied 2023-24 but no general strike).
- **Independent media (Mosaïque FM, Nawaat)** — pressured under Decree 54.
- **No effective Western pressure** — IMF deal collapsed 2023, but no rights conditionality enforced.

### Open empirical questions
- Why did Tunisia's exception fail where Spain/Portugal/Greece's didn't (compare 1970s-80s transition consolidation)?
- Was the absent Constitutional Court (never seated after 2014) the proximate cause, or symptom?
- Does the Cordall "disengagement" frame hold up against the "aggrandizement" frame empirically?

## BLR (Belarus, Lukashenko 1994-)

### Trigger events
- **July 1994**: Lukashenko elected president (anti-corruption populist).
- **May 1995 referendum**: gives president right to dissolve parliament; Russian made co-official; Soviet-era symbols restored.
- **November 1996 referendum** (constitutional): super-presidential system, extends Lukashenko's term to 2001, decree-power, control of state budget. **Widely seen as fraudulent.**
- **Oct 2004 referendum**: abolishes presidential term limits — "president for life" enabled.
- **2010 election**: opposition crackdown; ~700 detained election night.
- **2020 August election**: Lukashenko claims 80%; Tikhanovskaya runs in husband Sergei's place after his jailing; mass protests Aug-Dec 2020; ~35,000+ detained.
- **2022**: Belarus territory used in Russian invasion of Ukraine; tightens dependence on Putin.

### Captures, in order
1. **1995-1996 Parliament**: First by referendum (1995 dissolution right), then by 1996 referendum that replaces 13th Supreme Soviet with appointed bicameral National Assembly.
2. **1996 Judiciary**: Reorganized by constitutional referendum; Constitutional Court judges majority-appointed by president; lower court chairs presidentially appointed.
3. **1996-2000 Electoral commissions**: CEC made fully loyal; opposition systematically barred from ballots.
4. **2004 Term limits removed** — completes super-presidential vertical.
5. **2020- Security state**: KGB + GUBOPiK + presidentially-controlled militia; ~3,500 political prisoners by 2023 (Viasna count).
6. **2022 Constitution**: new referendum reduces presidential limit to two terms but enshrines All-Belarusian People's Assembly (loyalist body) above parliament.

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Bedford, S. (2025/2026). "Explaining the mass protests in Belarus in 2020: What role did civil society play?"** *Democratization* (online first, doi 10.1080/13510347.2025.2467459). Interviews with exiled Belarusians 2023; CSOs played important but mixed role; many became indistinguishable from broader protest movement; forced into exile after late-2020 repression.
- **Ioffe, G., & Yarashevich, V. (2011). "Debating Belarus: An Economy in Comparative Perspective."** *Eurasian Geography and Economics* — economic-rents underpinning of regime survival.
- **Korosteleva, E. (2012). "Belarusian Foreign Policy in a Time of Crisis."** Still cited.
- **Marin, A. (2020-2023)** writing on Lukashenko's post-2020 dependence on Russia.
- **Way, L. A.** comparative work on post-Soviet competitive authoritarianism positions Belarus as the "full-authoritarian" pole — see *Pluralism by Default* (2015) and subsequent updates.

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **Sviatlana Tsikhanouskaya / United Transitional Cabinet** in exile (Vilnius). Maintains parallel-state functions for diaspora.
- **2020 protests**: largest in Belarusian history; ~500,000 in Minsk at peak; lasted ~4 months; fully crushed by Dec 2020.
- **Viasna Human Rights Center** — documents political prisoners; founder Ales Bialiatski jailed (2022 Nobel laureate from prison).
- **Cyber Partisans** — hacker-collective opposition; sabotaged railway systems 2022.
- **Western sanctions** — extensive but largely symbolic; Russian economic backstop neutralizes.

### Open empirical questions
- What is the actual size of the regime's coercive apparatus, and what are its breakpoints?
- Does Lukashenko's succession (he's 70+ in 2026) produce regime continuity or fracture?
- Belarus as the "negative model" — what would the Bukelization trajectory look like in 25 years if SLV follows BLR mechanics?

## IND (India, Modi 2014-) — slow-burn variant

### Trigger events
- **May 2014**: Modi/BJP win 282/543 Lok Sabha seats on 31% vote share; first single-party majority since 1984.
- **2017 UP state election**: BJP supermajority in India's largest state; Yogi Adityanath as CM.
- **2019**: BJP re-elected with 303 seats. Article 370 (Kashmir autonomy) revoked Aug 2019; Citizenship Amendment Act Dec 2019.
- **2020 anti-CAA protests** suppressed; UAPA used against student activists, journalists.
- **2021 IT Rules**: tighten platform liability; raid on BBC offices (2023) after Modi documentary.
- **2023-2024**: Opposition INDIA bloc forms; 2024 election BJP loses majority (240/543), governs in coalition (NDA 293). V-Dem keeps "electoral autocracy" classification.

### Captures, in order
1. **2014-2024 Executive aggrandizement (slow)**: no single capture moment; instead, Khaitan's "thousand cuts." Centralization of decision-making in PMO; weakening of cabinet collective responsibility; sidelining of parliamentary committees.
2. **2016-2024 Independent agencies**: ED, CBI, Income Tax used disproportionately against opposition; Election Commission appointments politicized after 2023 law removing CJI from selection panel.
3. **2018-2024 Judiciary**: master-of-the-roster controversies; collegium-vs-NJAC dispute; key cases (Article 370, Babri Masjid, CAA challenge) deferred or favorable to government.
4. **2014-2024 Media**: ownership concentration (Adani-NDTV acquisition 2022); IT Rules + fact-check unit; raids on independent outlets (NewsClick, BBC). V-Dem: India top-20 globally for media censorship 2014-2024.
5. **2019-2024 Federalism**: opposition state governments destabilized via governor / ED / CBI; Maharashtra (2022), Karnataka contests; 2019 Delhi statehood reduction.
6. **2024 Pre-election arrests**: Kejriwal (Delhi CM, AAP), Soren (Jharkhand CM, JMM) arrested during campaign.

### Key recent papers (2020-2026)
- **Khaitan, T. (2020). "Killing a Constitution with a Thousand Cuts: Executive Aggrandizement and Party-State Fusion in India."** *Law & Ethics of Human Rights* 14(1): 49-95. **Foundational framework** — three accountability strands (vertical/diagonal/horizontal), each cut incrementally.
- **Ganguly, S., Diamond, L., & Plattner, M. (eds.) (2024). *Democratic Backsliding: An Indian Case Study.*** Various contributions.
- **Tudor, M., & Slater, D. (2021). "Nationalism, Authoritarianism, and Democracy: Historical Lessons from South and Southeast Asia."** *Perspectives on Politics* 19(3).
- **Vaishnav, M., Mehta, P. B., et al., Carnegie India / CASI Penn (2020-2024)** — empirical institutional-decline tracking.
- **Jaffrelot, C. (2021). *Modi's India: Hindu Nationalism and the Rise of Ethnic Democracy.*** Princeton UP. Comprehensive book-length account.
- **Sociocultural Component of India's Democratic Backsliding (2025).** *Asian Studies Review* 50(1) (Taylor & Francis).
- **V-Dem (2024) Democracy Report**: India classified electoral autocracy since 2018; 20% decline in judicial autonomy indicators 2014-2024; among top-20 countries for media censorship.

### Resistance / counter-mobilization
- **INDIA bloc** (28 opposition parties; Congress, DMK, TMC, SP, AAP, etc.) — formed 2023, won 234 seats 2024 (vs BJP 240).
- **Federal states** — opposition-held TN, Karnataka (post-2023), Kerala, WB, Punjab, Delhi, Jharkhand, etc. ~45% of population.
- **Supreme Court** — issues independent rulings (electoral bonds struck down Feb 2024, Article 370 upheld), uneven but not fully captured.
- **Independent media (The Wire, Scroll, Caravan, NewsClick (raided 2023), digital outlets)** — survive but under legal/tax pressure.
- **Farmers' protests (2020-2021)** — forced repeal of farm laws; rare successful resistance.

### Open empirical questions
- Does the 2024 coalition government slow or reverse backsliding, or is the institutional damage path-dependent?
- Khaitan's slow-burn model: empirically, how does this differ in trajectory shape from supermajority cases (HUN, SLV)?

# Cross-case mechanism patterns surfaced by literature

1. **Constitutional Court capture is usually first (HUN 2010-13, POL 2015, SLV 2021, TUR 2010 + 2017, VEN 2004, BLR 1996).** Tunisia's exception (TUN 2021 self-coup with no Court to block) confirms the rule. India is the only case where the apex court is not yet fully captured — and India is the slow-burn case.
2. **Supermajority OR judicial-vacuum is the enabling condition.** Where Article-VIII / Article-80 / Constitutional-Chamber exists with independent membership, backsliding requires either a 2/3 vote (HUN) or extra-constitutional move (POL refusal-to-seat, SLV May-1 dismissal, TUN self-coup). Where the Court is absent or already-loyal (BLR 1996, VEN 2004), the pace accelerates.
3. **Media capture is via ownership not censorship.** Hungary (Fidesz-aligned oligarchs), India (Adani-NDTV, Reliance), Turkey (Demirören acquisitions), Poland (state-media transformation) — direct censorship is the exception; oligarchic acquisition is the rule.
4. **Civil society capture follows judicial capture, never precedes it.** "Foreign agent" laws appear consistently after courts are neutralized.
5. **Electoral mechanics matter enormously.** All cases retain elections; capture is of administration (CEC), districting, candidate qualification, media access — not vote-counting per se (with BLR/VEN as exceptions where outright tally fraud documented).
