# Bukelization — Theoretical Canonical Literature

Pattern internally labeled "Bukelization": libdem starts ≥0.30, drops ≥0.30 over a 10-year window, monotonic, no military coup, no interstate war. Confirming corpus: SLV/HUN/TUR/VEN/POL/TUN/BLR + IND (slow-burn 18-24 yr). This document maps that empirical shape to the canonical academic literature.

## Dominant academic terms

The literature has converged on a small cluster of overlapping terms. None is one-to-one with our 7-country shape; ours is best described as a **specific empirical instantiation** of the broader phenomenon. Ranked by fit to our pattern:

1. **Autocratization (Third Wave)** — Lührmann & Lindberg (2019). Best fit. Explicitly defined as gradual, mostly-legal democratic decline starting from democratic baseline, not coup-driven. V-Dem operationalizes it on libdem index (our exact measure). Our 7-country pattern is essentially an "extreme tail" subset of the third wave.
2. **Democratic backsliding** — Bermeo (2016). Umbrella term. Distinguishes three modes: promissory coups, **executive aggrandizement** (closest to our cases), and strategic election manipulation. Our "no military coup" constraint isolates the executive-aggrandizement subtype.
3. **Constitutional retrogression** — Ginsburg & Huq (2018). Explicit contrast with "authoritarian reversion" (rapid collapse). Retrogression = subtle, incremental erosion of three institutional predicates (competitive elections, speech/association rights, rule of law) — matches our sequential sub-indicator capture.
4. **Autocratic legalism** — Scheppele (2018). Captures the *mechanism* (legal/electoral mandate weaponized against democracy itself). Hungary-archetypal; ports cleanly to SLV/POL/TUN.
5. **Abusive constitutionalism** — Landau (2013). Sister concept to Scheppele's; emphasizes constitutional amendment/replacement as the lever. Strong fit for VEN/HUN/TUN.
6. **Competitive authoritarianism** — Levitsky & Way (2002, 2010, 2025). Describes the *destination* state — meaningful elections + serious incumbent abuse → real but unfair competition. Our 7 cases land here at libdem ~0.10-0.20.
7. **Stealth authoritarianism** — Varol (2015). Sub-constitutional legal tools. Adjacent but narrower.
8. **Illiberal democracy** — Zakaria (1997). Originating term, now considered too coarse; subsumed by the above.

**Best-fit consensus term for our pattern: "incumbent-led autocratization episode within the third wave, via executive aggrandizement / autocratic legalism."** Lührmann & Lindberg's framework is the one our V-Dem measurement strategy most directly inherits.

## Top 14 papers (sorted by relevance)

### 1. Lührmann, A. & Lindberg, S.I. (2019). "A third wave of autocratization is here: what is new about it?" *Democratization* 26(7): 1095-1113.
- First comprehensive empirical overview of all autocratization episodes 1900–2018 using V-Dem.
- **Why relevant:** Defines the wave our 7 cases belong to; uses identical V-Dem libdem framework; explicit claim that this wave is "gradual and surreptitious," "under legal façade," "democracies not autocracies." Their case list overlaps heavily with ours (HUN, TUR, VEN, POL all named).
- Key finding: 75 autocratization episodes since 1994; **almost all are former democracies**, not autocracies tightening.

### 2. Sato, Y., Lundstedt, M., Morrison, K., Boese, V.A., & Lindberg, S.I. (2022). "Institutional Order in Episodes of Autocratization." V-Dem Working Paper #133.
- **CRITICAL FOR OUR SUB-INDICATOR FINDING.** Tests pair-wise domination of institutional decline across 31 V-Dem variables over 69 autocratization episodes (1900-2021).
- **Why relevant:** Directly tests order-of-capture, which is exactly our empirical claim. Their finding: institutional decay starts with **horizontal accountability** (legislature, judiciary), then **diagonal** (media, civil society), then **vertical** (elections). This is *slightly different* from our observed order (elections → media → judiciary → civil society) — worth checking whether our window definition picks up "free-and-fair" before "judicial constraints" because of measurement timing or because our cases genuinely break the pattern. **Action: read full paper before locking sub-indicator narrative.**
- Pattern stronger "in countries with low democratic stock and during the third wave."

### 3. Bermeo, N. (2016). "On Democratic Backsliding." *Journal of Democracy* 27(1): 5-19.
- Foundational typology. Distinguishes (a) classic coups, (b) **executive aggrandizement** — elected leaders disable horizontal checks while preserving electoral facade, (c) strategic election manipulation.
- **Why relevant:** Our 7-country shape *is* executive aggrandizement. "No military coup" filter explicitly excludes Bermeo's category (a); the slow libdem decay is exactly her category (b).

### 4. Scheppele, K.L. (2018). "Autocratic Legalism." *University of Chicago Law Review* 85: 545-583.
- Defines autocratic legalism as electoral mandate + constitutional/legal change in service of illiberal agenda. Hungary as archetype.
- **Why relevant:** The mechanism narrative for HUN/POL/TUN/SLV. "Frankenstate" concept. Quote: *"come to power through elections, then slowly use legal methods to eliminate liberal democracy by targeting opposition, controlling media, going after critics, and changing election laws."* That is a sequencing claim that overlaps ours.

### 5. Levitsky, S. & Way, L. (2025). "The New Competitive Authoritarianism." *Journal of Democracy*.
- Updates their 2002/2010 framework. Argues competitive authoritarianism is now diffusing into countries with **strong democratic institutions** (the original framework was about weak-institution post-Cold-War states).
- **Why relevant:** This is the most current canonical update. Our SLV/HUN/POL/TUN cases are exactly the "diffusion" they describe.

### 6. Ginsburg, T. & Huq, A.Z. (2018). *How to Save a Constitutional Democracy.* University of Chicago Press.
- Distinguishes **authoritarian reversion** (fast, total) from **constitutional retrogression** (slow, partial). Argues retrogression is the dominant modern pathway and constitutional safeguards against it are weak.
- **Why relevant:** Our shape definition (libdem ≥0.30 → drop ≥0.30 over 10 yrs, monotonic) is structurally identical to their retrogression construct. Three institutional predicates they track (elections, speech/association, rule of law) map to our sub-indicators.

### 7. Landau, D. (2013). "Abusive Constitutionalism." *UC Davis Law Review* 47(1): 189-260.
- Constitutional amendment/replacement as a tool of democratic erosion. Companion to Scheppele.
- **Why relevant:** Direct fit for VEN (1999 constituent assembly), HUN (2011 Fundamental Law), TUN (2022 referendum). Locks down constitutional-capture sub-indicator interpretation.

### 8. Haggard, S. & Kaufman, R. (2021). *Backsliding: Democratic Regress in the Contemporary World.* Cambridge University Press.
- 16-case cross-national comparison. Includes HUN, POL, TUR, VEN, plus others.
- **Why relevant:** Directly tests cross-country backsliding patterns — closest published analogue to our cross-country empirical work. Argues "leaders with authoritarian tendencies gradually undermine democracy through repeated legal and institutional changes, rather than abrupt ruptures."

### 9. Waldner, D. & Lust, E. (2018). "Unwelcome Change: Coming to Terms with Democratic Backsliding." *Annual Review of Political Science* 21: 93-113.
- Methodological/conceptual cleanup of the backsliding concept. Key reference for "what counts as backsliding."
- **Why relevant:** Essential citation for our operationalization (continuous libdem decline as the definition). Notes that alternative coding schemes change the population of backsliding cases — relevant for our case-list robustness.

### 10. Maerz, S.F., Edgell, A.B., Wilson, M.C., Hellmeier, S., & Lindberg, S.I. (2024). "Episodes of regime transformation." *Journal of Peace Research* 61(6): 967-984. [+ companion ERT dataset paper, SSRN 3781485]
- Introduces the **ERT (Episodes of Regime Transformation) dataset** — 680 episodes 1900-2019 from V-Dem. Differentiates 4 macro-types × 10 patterns.
- **Why relevant:** This is the dataset our pattern-extraction should be benchmarked against. **Action: pull ERT, check whether all 7 of our cases register as ERT episodes and whether the 10 sub-patterns include something that maps tightly to our shape.**

### 11. Levitsky, S. & Ziblatt, D. (2018). *How Democracies Die.* Crown.
- Trade book with serious comparative core. "Soft guardrails" / norm erosion + formal institutional changes.
- **Why relevant:** Best lay-readable framing. Quote: *"democracy no longer ends with a bang—in a revolution or military coup—but with a whimper."* That's our pattern in one sentence.

### 12. Cassani, A. & Tomini, L. (2020). "Reversing regimes and concepts: from democratization to autocratization." *European Political Science* 19: 272-287. Also Cassani & Tomini (2019), "Post-Cold War autocratization," *Italian PSR*.
- Conceptual mapping of autocratization typology. Argues participation, contestation, executive limitation are the three dimensions of variance.
- **Why relevant:** Useful theoretical scaffolding for distinguishing our "Bukelization" subtype from other autocratization modes (e.g., authoritarian-to-more-authoritarian closure).

### 13. V-Dem Institute (2025). *Democracy Report 2025: 25 Years of Autocratization — Democracy Trumped?* Nord et al.
- Latest authoritative state-of-the-world. 72% of world population now under autocracy; 45 countries in ongoing autocratization episodes.
- **Why relevant:** Authoritative current count. Identifies HUN, BLR, RUS, SRB, IND as ongoing cases; flags US as "fastest evolving episode of autocratization the USA has been through in modern history" — **possible 8th case for us to test.**

### 14. Corrales, J. (2022/2023). "Venezuela's Autocratization, 1999-2021" (chapter in *When Democracy Breaks*, OUP) + Mainwaring & Corrales-related work, "Backsliding by surprise: the rise of Chavismo" (Cambridge, 2023).
- Authoritative case study of VEN. Identifies three mechanisms: erosion of liberal democracy institutions, distortion of participatory democracy, declines in minimal democracy.
- **Why relevant:** Provides the canonical narrative for one of our 7 confirming cases. Notes the institutional capture sequence: Constituent Assembly → courts → CNE → bureaucracy → military → oil company → legislature.

## Mechanism literature

Why does this happen? Literature converges on a multi-factor model:

1. **Economic-crisis trigger + party-system collapse.** Hungary 2008 financial crisis → Fidesz 2010 supermajority. Venezuela 1990s collapse of AD/COPEI → Chávez 1998. Peru 1990 hyperinflation → Fujimori. The pattern is: weak/discredited mainstream parties + acute shock → opening for populist outsider with anti-system mandate. (Haggard & Kaufman 2021; LSE Business Review on financial crises and right-wing populism; CEPR working papers on financial crises as populism drivers.)
2. **Populist ideology + majoritarian framing.** "Will of the people" rhetoric used to delegitimize horizontal checks. (Mounk 2018; Müller's *What Is Populism?*; Mudde & Rovira Kaltwasser.)
3. **Polarization.** Levitsky & Ziblatt emphasize partisan elite defection from democratic norms — opposition fails to coalesce in defense of the system because polarization makes joint action with "the other side" feel worse than autocratization.
4. **Self-preservation (executive personal stakes).** Netanyahu corruption trials; Bukele's prior legal exposure; Orbán's family business ties. Several authors (e.g., Cohen-Almagor on Israel) argue backsliding is "driven less by ideology than by self-preservation."
5. **Diffusion / learning.** Levitsky & Way 2025 explicitly frame the new wave as **autocrats learning from each other** — Hungary's playbook adopted in Poland, Turkey, El Salvador. Scheppele's "Frankenstate" concept emphasizes copy-paste of legal techniques.
6. **Weak constitutional safeguards.** Ginsburg & Huq argue formal checks underperform against retrogression because the tools (amendment, judicial appointment, electoral law) are themselves discretionary and capturable.

## Open theoretical debates

1. **Is this a "new wave" or a continuation of the 3rd wave's troughs?** Lührmann & Lindberg argue this is genuinely the third autocratization wave (after interwar and Cold-War). Diamond's earlier framing as "recession" is softer. Cassani & Tomini sit in between. **For our purposes: the wave framing is dominant and we should adopt it.**
2. **What is the right sequence of institutional capture?** Sato et al. 2022 say horizontal → diagonal → vertical. Other V-Dem work (Maerz, Edgell) finds diagonal-first patterns. Field guides (Bermeo, Scheppele) emphasize elections-then-courts-then-media. **Our 7-country finding (elections → media → judiciary → civil society) is testable against ERT pair-wise dominance — and is empirically actionable.**
3. **Reversibility.** Can a country exit Bukelization? Poland 2023 (Tusk's coalition unseating PiS) is the natural test case. Carnegie's 2025 "Democratic Recovery After Significant Backsliding" paper attempts a typology. Tunisia's reversibility is open. Our POL case-window ending 2023 is exactly this question.
4. **Public-support paradox.** Bukele has supermajority approval *during* democratic dismantlement. ("Millennial authoritarianism," *Journal of Democracy*.) Does popular consent change the normative status of the autocratization? Empirically: no, libdem still falls. Normatively: contested.
5. **Is competitive authoritarianism a stable equilibrium or a way-station to full autocracy?** VEN slid past it (now electoral autocracy / closed autocracy). HUN, POL stayed inside it. TUR is borderline. SLV trajectory unknown.
6. **"Democracy with adjectives" critique.** Collier & Levitsky's classic warning: stacking modifiers ("illiberal," "stealth," "competitive," "millennial," "managed") may signal conceptual stretching rather than analytic progress. Our pattern-naming should be aware of this.
