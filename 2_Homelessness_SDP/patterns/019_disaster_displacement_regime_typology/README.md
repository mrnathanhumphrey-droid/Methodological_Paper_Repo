## Original 4-regime discovery (2026-05-25)

## One line
Countries cluster into 4 distinct disaster-displacement regimes based on (a) which hazard channel dominates and (b) how event sizes distribute across years. PAK is uniquely a bimodal-mega-flood regime; IND is steady-high-flood; PHL/VNM/MOZ are storm-dominant; BGD is mixed-flood-storm.

## The 4 regimes

### Regime 1 — Bimodal-mega-flood (PAK only in current corpus)
- Indus river geography: steep Himalayan gradient + glacial melt + monsoon belt + flat Indus plain
- 3 mega-flood events (2010, 2012, 2022); most other years sub-million
- Flood max/median = **65.1×** (extreme bimodality)
- Storm channel essentially absent (no typhoon exposure)
- Structurally unique; not a class of countries

### Regime 2 — Steady high-flood (IND)
- Multiple major river systems (Ganges, Brahmaputra, Indus tributaries)
- 14 of 17 years exceed 1M flood-displaced
- Max/median = 3.8× (steady, not bimodal)
- Annual cumulative load is huge (~46M total flood-IDP 2008-2024)
- Storm component also significant (cyclones in Odisha, Bay of Bengal)

### Regime 3 — Storm/typhoon-dominant (PHL, VNM, MOZ)
- Tropical cyclone belts (Pacific typhoons / Indian Ocean cyclones)
- Storm channel >> flood channel (84-97% storm share)
- PHL especially: 16 of 17 years exceed 1M storm-displaced; **58M cumulative storm-IDP**
- Storm max/median 2.5-13× (relatively steady)
- Geographically distinct from delta-flood countries

### Regime 4 — Mixed flood-storm (BGD)
- Coastal delta + monsoon + Bay of Bengal cyclones (Sundarbans)
- Both channels firing; flood 4.0M / storm 6.2M acute
- 2 mega-flood years + 6 mega-storm years
- Distinct from PAK (bimodal) and IND (steady) — sits between regimes

## Why it stands out
Just like the [[INDEX]] meta-pattern of conflict-types (formal-army / predator-militia / irregular-insurgency / bimodal-flood), this is a **typology of mechanisms** for disaster-displacement. The 4 regimes have:
- Different governing physical processes (river floods vs cyclones vs glacial-monsoon)
- Different temporal structures (bimodal mega-events vs steady annual vs typhoon-season)
- Different aid implications (budget for once-a-decade catastrophe vs annual recurring vs season-by-season)
- Different climate-attribution stories (glacial melt vs sea-surface warming → typhoon intensity vs monsoon shifts)

If this regime-typology firms up across more countries (Caribbean cyclones, US Gulf coast, South Pacific atolls, Central American storms), it complements the conflict-type typology and **gives the methodology paper a second meta-argument**: corpus heterogeneity isn't noise on either the conflict or the disaster side; both decompose into a small number of distinct types.
